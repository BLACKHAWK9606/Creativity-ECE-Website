Introduction

Art is one of the most natural and expressive forms of creativity for young children. Through drawing, painting, sculpting, and collage, children explore colours, textures, emotions, and ideas. Art allows them to make sense of the world around them and communicate what they cannot yet express with words. The process â€” not the product â€” is where the richest learning occurs. As children engage in art, they develop fine motor skills, problem-solving abilities, and self-confidence. Art also supports emotional regulation and gives children a safe space to express their feelings, thoughts, and identities.

Theories and Perspectives

Art in early childhood is supported by Howard Gardner's theory of multiple intelligences, particularly visual-spatial intelligence, which recognizes art as a vital way of knowing and expressing. The Reggio Emilia approach also emphasizes that children use "a hundred languages" â€” including art â€” to construct meaning and communicate. Lev Vygotsky believed that creativity develops through social interactions, with children learning artistic expression by observing, imitating, and collaborating. These perspectives highlight that art is not an isolated activity, but a deeply social, cognitive, and emotional process embedded in children's everyday experiences.

Resources, Materials, and Digital Tools

To foster creativity through art, educators should offer a wide variety of open-ended materials. These include crayons, oil pastels, paint, glue, recycled cardboard, clay, fabric scraps, and natural materials like leaves and stones. Digital tools such as drawing apps (e.g., Doodle Buddy, Tux Paint) can also expand artistic possibilities. Having an inviting art space that children can access independently encourages self-expression. Materials should be displayed in accessible, organised ways that invite exploration and experimentation. The key is to value the process and allow children to make choices and take creative risks.
