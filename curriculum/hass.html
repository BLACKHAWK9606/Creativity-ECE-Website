Humanities and Social Sciences (HASS)

Exploring people, places, cultures, and relationships to build empathy and belonging.

Introduction

HASS in early childhood focuses on children's understanding of people, places, cultures, relationships, and the passage of time. Through creative experiences in storytelling, role-play, mapping, and exploration of artefacts, young children build empathy and a sense of belonging. They begin to see themselves as part of a community and develop awareness of diversity, fairness, and history. Creativity allows children to imagine different perspectives, express identity, and ask meaningful questions about their world. In this area, play becomes a vehicle for social inquiry, and children learn not just facts â€” but how to be thoughtful, active participants in society.

Creativity Theories & Perspectives

Bronfenbrenner's ecological systems theory shows how children's development is shaped by the social and cultural environments they live in â€” which aligns with HASS topics like community, family, and roles. Vygotsky's theory emphasizes the role of shared dialogue and culture in constructing knowledge. Gardner's interpersonal and intrapersonal intelligences are engaged as children explore relationships and reflect on themselves. Jerome Bruner advocated for the use of narrative in making sense of the human experience. These perspectives support the view that creativity in HASS arises through imaginative play, storytelling, and reflective conversation grounded in real-world experiences.

Resources & Technologies

To creatively explore HASS, educators can provide:

  • Maps, globes, and photos of local and global environments.
  • Cultural artefacts: clothing, instruments, utensils, tools, and flags.
  • Storytelling props: dolls, puppets, emotion cards, and "small world" scenes.
  • Community role-play materials: post office, doctor's kit, supermarket setup.
  • Books on identity and belonging: e.g., Whoever You Are, I'm Australian Too, All Are Welcome.
  • Digital tools: Google Earth, Barefoot World Atlas, Little Explorers apps for cultural exploration.

These tools support empathy, identity-building, and global citizenship while encouraging children to ask, imagine, and express.

Learning Experiences

Learning Experience 1: "Family Story Circles" (Age Group: 2â€“3 years)

Children are invited to bring in or draw a picture of their family. During a group story circle, each child shares something special about their family or home. Educators use books and emotion dolls to model cultural respect and listening. Children create a classroom "community quilt" with symbols representing their family. This activity supports self-identity, empathy, and inclusion.

Learning Experience 2: "Post Office Play" (Age Group: 3â€“5 years)

A pretend post office is set up with envelopes, stamps, boxes, and mailbags. Children create letters or drawings to deliver around the classroom. Roles include post officer, mail carrier, and customer. Educators introduce vocabulary (address, stamp, delivery), numeracy (house numbers), and storytelling. This play builds communication, civic understanding, and cooperative creativity.
